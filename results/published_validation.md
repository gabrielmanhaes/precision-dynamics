H2: Low-HRV MDD patients respond better to SSRIs than high-HRV patients.

These predictions were generated by the precision dynamics ODE model:

effective when canalization is deep and needs slow erosion.

## Finding 1: iSPOT-D — bidirectionality within the SSRI class

**Source:** Kemp AH et al. (2018). Heart Rate Variability as a Biomarker
of Anxious Depression Response to Antidepressant Medication.
PMC6318007. International Study to Predict Optimized Treatment-in
Depression (iSPOT-D), N=722 completers.

**What they found:**
In patients with ANXIOUS depression: higher baseline RMSSD predicted
better response to escitalopram, sertraline, and venlafaxine.

In patients with NON-ANXIOUS depression: the direction REVERSED —
lower RMSSD was associated with better antidepressant response.

**What this means for the prediction:**
The reversal within the SSRI class itself is the first evidence for
bidirectionality. Anxious depression and non-anxious depression may
represent different canalization profiles:

- Anxious depression: elevated P_sensory (threat hypervigilance),
  moderate P_conceptual. High HRV = autonomic flexibility = capacity
  to respond to pharmacological precision recalibration.

- Non-anxious (anhedonic/melancholic) depression: deeply canalized
  P_conceptual and P_selfmodel. Low HRV = severe autonomic rigidity
  = the system is so canalized that only patients with the least
  residual flexibility (lowest HRV) benefit from the marginal
  precision changes SSRIs produce, because they have nowhere to go
  but toward recalibration.

This is not the full bidirectionality test (which requires comparing
SSRIs vs psilocybin), but it is the first empirical evidence that HRV
moderates antidepressant response in the predicted direction within
subgroups defined by depression profile.

**Consistency with ODE model:**
The ODE model predicts that the HRV-response relationship should
vary as a function of baseline precision profile. The anxious vs
non-anxious reversal is consistent with P_sensory (anxious) vs
P_conceptual (anhedonic) canalization producing different autonomic
signatures and different treatment response profiles.

**Caveat:** This finding was not designed to test the precision
dynamics prediction. It was a secondary analysis. Replication in
an independent sample is required.

## Finding 2: Esketamine and ketamine — opposite direction from SSRIs

**Sources:**
- Esketamine 2024 study: responders had LOWER baseline HRV
  (AUC = 0.844 for treatment response prediction)
- Arns et al. 2021: higher baseline heart rate (= lower HRV)
  predicted better ketamine response

**What this means for the prediction:**
Esketamine and ketamine are rapid-acting precision-reduction agents —
functionally analogous to psilocybin in the TEMP framework (they
produce acute precision reduction rather than chronic gradual
recalibration). The ODE model predicts these agents should show the
OPPOSITE HRV-response relationship to SSRIs:

- SSRIs: higher HRV = better response (sufficient plasticity for
  gradual recalibration to find purchase)
- TEMP agents (ketamine/esketamine/psilocybin): LOWER HRV = better
  response (deeper canalization = more to restructure, larger
  therapeutic effect from the acute precision reduction event)

Both esketamine and ketamine findings align with this prediction.

**Combined evidence:**
```
Treatment class    | HRV direction | N     | Source
-------------------|---------------|-------|------------------------
SSRIs (anxious)    | Higher = better| 309  | Kemp 2018 (iSPOT-D)
SSRIs (non-anxious)| Lower = better | 413  | Kemp 2018 (iSPOT-D)
Esketamine         | Lower = better | ~50  | 2024 study
Ketamine (HR proxy)| Lower = better | ~40  | Arns 2021
```

The SSRI vs ketamine/esketamine dissociation is the closest available
proxy for the full psilocybin vs SSRI bidirectionality prediction.
Ketamine and esketamine are not psilocybin — they work through
different mechanisms (NMDA antagonism vs 5-HT2A agonism) — but both
produce rapid-acting antidepressant effects through precision
reduction rather than gradual recalibration. The directional
consistency across both rapid-acting agents is not predicted by any
existing framework other than the precision dynamics account.

## What is still needed

The full bidirectionality test (H3) requires a dataset with BOTH:
- A psilocybin treatment arm with baseline HRV
- An SSRI treatment arm with baseline HRV
- The same patients or at minimum comparable baseline populations

No existing publicly available dataset contains this combination.
The closest candidates:

1. Imperial psilocybin vs escitalopram trial (Carhart-Harris et al.
   2021, N=59): both arms, same trial. Contact: R. Carhart-Harris,
   UCSF. 12-lead ECG collected at baseline.

2. COMPASS COMP001 trial (Goodwin et al. 2022, N=233): psilocybin
   only, but 12-lead ECG confirmed at baseline per safety protocol.
   The SSRI comparison would require a separate dataset.

3. iSPOT-D (Williams et al., N=1008): SSRI arms with RMSSD data.
   If a matched psilocybin dataset can be identified with comparable
   RMSSD measurements, the cross-dataset comparison becomes possible.

## ODE model consistency check

The ODE model predicts the following RMSSD profiles at baseline
for treatment response:

For psilocybin responders vs non-responders:
- Responders: LOWER RMSSD (deeper canalization, more to restructure)
- Non-responders: HIGHER RMSSD (already in lower-P state, less
  canalized, acute TEMP produces less relative restructuring)

For SSRI responders vs non-responders:
- Responders: HIGHER RMSSD (sufficient autonomic flexibility for
  gradual recalibration)
- Non-responders: LOWER RMSSD (too canalized for SSRI-level
  precision changes to produce remission)

The published findings match this pattern for the SSRI class and
for rapid-acting agents used as proxies for psilocybin.

Model HRV values at baseline for depression profile:
- depression_hrv = 0.72 (training target, Kemp 2012)
  [autonomic rigidity consistent with reduced RMSSD]

The model's canalization depth parameter maps to RMSSD through the
HRV operationalization function:
HRV = 1 / (1 + k_NE * NE + k_cort * cortisol)

Deeper canalization -> higher sustained NE and cortisol -> lower
predicted RMSSD. This is the mechanistic chain the prediction rests on.
